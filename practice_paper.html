Q1 Descriptive Statistics & Central Limit Theorem
6/6 Papers5 Marks

a) A quality control engineer recorded the following breaking strengths (in kg) of 10 randomly selected steel cables from a production line:

12, 7, 25, 18, 30, 22, 42, 15, 35, 28

i)Compute the mean, sample standard deviation, and five-number summary (Min, Q1, Q2, Q3, Max). [2 Marks]
ii)Calculate IQR and identify any potential outliers using the 1.5×IQR rule. [1 Mark]
iii)Based on Mean vs Median, comment on the symmetry/skewness of the data. [0.5 Mark]

b) The breaking strength of cables from a large factory is normally distributed with \(\mu = 45\) kg and \(\sigma = 12\) kg. A random sample of \(n = 36\) cables is selected. Find \(P(42 < \bar{X} < 48)\). [1.5 Marks]

✔️ Complete Solution

Part (a)

Step 1 — Sort the Data

Original: 12, 7, 25, 18, 30, 22, 42, 15, 35, 28

Sorted: \(7,\ 12,\ 15,\ 18,\ 22,\ 25,\ 28,\ 30,\ 35,\ 42\)     \(n = 10\)

Step 2 — Mean
\[\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} = \frac{7+12+15+18+22+25+28+30+35+42}{10} = \frac{234}{10} = \mathbf{23.4}\]
Step 3 — Five-Number Summary

\(\text{Min} = 7, \quad \text{Max} = 42\)

\(Q_2\) (Median): Average of 5th and 6th value \(= \dfrac{22+25}{2} = \mathbf{23.5}\)

\(Q_1\) position: \(0.25 \times (n+1) = 0.25 \times 11 = 2.75\text{th value}\)

\(Q_1 = 12 + 0.75 \times (15-12) = 12 + 2.25 = \mathbf{14.25}\)

\(Q_3\) position: \(0.75 \times (n+1) = 0.75 \times 11 = 8.25\text{th value}\)

\(Q_3 = 30 + 0.25 \times (35-30) = 30 + 1.25 = \mathbf{31.25}\)

Step 4 — Sample Standard Deviation
\[s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]

Squared deviations \((x_i - 23.4)^2\):

(7−23.4)²=268.96  |  (12−23.4)²=129.96  |  (15−23.4)²=70.56
(18−23.4)²=29.16  |  (22−23.4)²=1.96    |  (25−23.4)²=2.56
(28−23.4)²=21.16  |  (30−23.4)²=43.56  |  (35−23.4)²=134.56
(42−23.4)²=345.96

\(\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2 = 1048.4\)

\[s^2 = \frac{1048.4}{9} = 116.49 \quad \Rightarrow \quad s = \sqrt{116.49} \approx \mathbf{10.79}\]
Step 5 — IQR & Outlier Detection
\[IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 = 31.25 - 14.25 = 17\]

\(\text{Lower bound} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times IQR = 14.25 - 25.5 = -11.25\)

\(\text{Upper bound} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times IQR = 31.25 + 25.5 = 56.75\)

All values lie within \([-11.25,\ 56.75]\) → No outliers detected.

Step 6 — Skewness Comment

Mean \(= 23.4\) and Median \(= 23.5\)

Since Mean \(\approx\) Median, the data is approximately symmetric (very slight left skew).

Part (b) — CLT / Sampling Distribution

Given

\(\mu = 45,\quad \sigma = 12,\quad n = 36\)

Step 1 — Standard Error of Mean
\[\sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{36}} = \frac{12}{6} = 2\]
Step 2 — Standardize Both Limits
\[Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]

\(Z_1 = \dfrac{42-45}{2} = -1.5\)     \(Z_2 = \dfrac{48-45}{2} = 1.5\)

Step 3 — Find Probability
\[P(42 < \bar{X} < 48) = P(-1.5 < Z < 1.5) = \Phi(1.5) - \Phi(-1.5)\]

\(= 0.9332 - (1 - 0.9332) = 0.9332 - 0.0668 = \mathbf{0.8664}\)

✅ Answers:   Mean = 23.4,   s ≈ 10.79,   Q1=14.25,   Q2=23.5,   Q3=31.25,   IQR=17,   No outliers.  |  Part b: P = 0.8664 (86.64%)

Q2 Bayes' Theorem & Probability Rules
6/6 Papers5 Marks

a) A pharmaceutical company manufactures a medicine in three factories A, B, and C. Factory A produces 50%, Factory B produces 30%, and Factory C produces 20% of the total output. The probabilities that a tablet produced is defective are 2%, 3%, and 5% for factories A, B, and C respectively. A tablet is picked at random and found to be defective. [3 Marks]

i)What is the probability that a randomly selected tablet is defective?
ii)What is the probability that the defective tablet came from Factory A?

b) For events A and B: \(P(A) = 0.40\), \(P(B) = 0.35\), \(P(A \cap B) = 0.12\). Find: \(P(A \cup B)\), \(P(A|B)\), \(P(B|A)\), and \(P(A' \cap B')\). [2 Marks]

✔️ Complete Solution

Part (a)

Given

\(P(A)=0.50,\ P(B)=0.30,\ P(C)=0.20\)

\(P(D|A)=0.02,\ P(D|B)=0.03,\ P(D|C)=0.05\)

Step 1 — Law of Total Probability
\[P(D) = P(D|A)\cdot P(A) + P(D|B)\cdot P(B) + P(D|C)\cdot P(C)\]

\(= (0.02)(0.50) + (0.03)(0.30) + (0.05)(0.20)\)

\(= 0.010 + 0.009 + 0.010 = \mathbf{0.029}\)

Step 2 — Apply Bayes' Theorem
\[P(A|D) = \frac{P(D|A)\cdot P(A)}{P(D)} = \frac{0.02 \times 0.50}{0.029} = \frac{0.010}{0.029} \approx \mathbf{0.3448}\]

There is a 34.48% probability the defective tablet came from Factory A.

Part (b)

Probability Rules Applied

1. Addition Rule:

\[P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) = 0.40 + 0.35 - 0.12 = \mathbf{0.63}\]

2. Conditional Probability \(P(A|B)\):

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{0.12}{0.35} = \mathbf{0.343}\]

3. Conditional Probability \(P(B|A)\):

\[P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} = \frac{0.12}{0.40} = \mathbf{0.30}\]

4. De Morgan's Law:

\[P(A' \cap B') = 1 - P(A \cup B) = 1 - 0.63 = \mathbf{0.37}\]
✅ P(D) = 0.029 | P(A|D) ≈ 0.3448 (34.48%) | P(A∪B)=0.63 | P(A|B)≈0.343 | P(B|A)=0.30 | P(A'∩B')=0.37

Q3 Naïve Bayes Classifier with Laplace Smoothing
6/6 Papers5 Marks

A company wants to classify customer reviews as Positive or Negative using a Naïve Bayes classifier. The training dataset is given below:

Review IDReview TextLabel
1great product quality servicePositive
2worst experience poor qualityNegative
3excellent service great valuePositive
4poor product terrible experienceNegative

Using Naïve Bayes with Laplace smoothing (\(\alpha = 1\)), classify the new review: "great service poor quality"

Show all working steps clearly.

✔️ Complete Solution — 5 Steps
Step 1 — Class Counts & Prior Probabilities

Total reviews = 4  |  Positive = 2  |  Negative = 2

\[P(\text{Positive}) = \frac{2}{4} = 0.5 \qquad P(\text{Negative}) = \frac{2}{4} = 0.5\]
Step 2 — Build Vocabulary

All unique words (V): {great, product, quality, service, worst, experience, poor, excellent, value, terrible}

\(|V| = 10\)

Positive class words: great(2), product(1), quality(1), service(2), excellent(1), value(1) → Total = 8

Negative class words: worst(1), experience(2), poor(2), quality(1), product(1), terrible(1) → Total = 8

Step 3 — Laplace Smoothed Likelihoods
\[P(w \mid C) = \frac{\text{count}(w, C) + 1}{\text{Total words in } C + |V|}\]

Test words: great, service, poor, quality

WordCount in PositiveCount in NegativeP(w|Positive)P(w|Negative)
great20(2+1)/(8+10) = 3/18(0+1)/(8+10) = 1/18
service203/181/18
poor021/183/18
quality112/182/18
Step 4 — Compute Posterior Scores

Score for Positive:

\[P(+)\times P(\text{great}|+)\times P(\text{service}|+)\times P(\text{poor}|+)\times P(\text{quality}|+)\] \[= 0.5 \times \frac{3}{18} \times \frac{3}{18} \times \frac{1}{18} \times \frac{2}{18} = 0.5 \times \frac{18}{104976} \approx 8.57 \times 10^{-5}\]

Score for Negative:

\[P(-)\times P(\text{great}|-)\times P(\text{service}|-)\times P(\text{poor}|-)\times P(\text{quality}|-)\] \[= 0.5 \times \frac{1}{18} \times \frac{1}{18} \times \frac{3}{18} \times \frac{2}{18} = 0.5 \times \frac{6}{104976} \approx 2.86 \times 10^{-5}\]
Step 5 — Compare & Classify

\(P(\text{Positive}|\text{review}) \approx 8.57 \times 10^{-5}\)

\(P(\text{Negative}|\text{review}) \approx 2.86 \times 10^{-5}\)

Since \(P(\text{Positive}) > P(\text{Negative})\)

✅ Classification: POSITIVE — The review "great service poor quality" is classified as Positive because the positive class score (8.57×10⁻⁵) is higher than negative (2.86×10⁻⁵), driven by strong positive words "great" and "service".

Q4 Normal Distribution & Z-scores
6/6 Papers5 Marks

The scores of students in an ISM exam are normally distributed with a mean of 68 and a standard deviation of 10. (\(X \sim N(68, 10^2)\))

i)Find the probability that a student scores less than 80. [1 Mark]
ii)Find the probability that a student scores between 55 and 80. [1.5 Marks]
iii)The top 5% of students qualify for the Dean's List. Find the minimum score needed. [1.5 Marks]
iv)A sample of 25 students is selected. Find \(P(\bar{X} > 70)\). [1 Mark]
✔️ Complete Solution
Key Formula
\[Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} = \frac{X - 68}{10}\]
Part i — P(X < 80)
\[Z = \frac{80 - 68}{10} = \frac{12}{10} = 1.2\]

\(P(X < 80) = P(Z < 1.2) = \Phi(1.2) = \mathbf{0.8849}\)

→ About 88.49% of students score below 80.

Part ii — P(55 < X < 80)

\(Z_1 = \dfrac{55 - 68}{10} = -1.3\)     \(Z_2 = \dfrac{80-68}{10} = 1.2\)

\[P(55 < X < 80) = \Phi(1.2) - \Phi(-1.3)\]

\(\Phi(-1.3) = 1 - \Phi(1.3) = 1 - 0.9032 = 0.0968\)

\[= 0.8849 - 0.0968 = \mathbf{0.7881}\]

→ 78.81% of students score between 55 and 80.

Part iii — Dean's List Cutoff (Top 5%)

Top 5% means \(P(X > x_0) = 0.05\) → \(P(Z > z_0) = 0.05\)

From Z-table: \(z_0 = 1.645\) (critical value for 95th percentile)

\[x_0 = \mu + z_0 \cdot \sigma = 68 + 1.645 \times 10 = 68 + 16.45 = \mathbf{84.45}\]

→ A student must score at least 84.45 to make the Dean's List.

Part iv — Sampling Distribution P(X̄ > 70)
\[\sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{25}} = \frac{10}{5} = 2\]

\(Z = \dfrac{70 - 68}{2} = 1.0\)

\[P(\bar{X} > 70) = P(Z > 1.0) = 1 - \Phi(1.0) = 1 - 0.8413 = \mathbf{0.1587}\]
✅ i) P(X<80) = 0.8849  |  ii) P(55<X<80) = 0.7881  |  iii) Dean's List cutoff = 84.45  |  iv) P(X̄>70) = 0.1587

Q5 Joint Probability Distribution
5/6 Papers5 Marks

Let \(X\) (number of failed servers) and \(Y\) (system load level) be discrete random variables with joint PMF:

\[f(x,y) = c(x + y) \quad \text{for } x \in \{0,1,2\},\; y \in \{1,2\}\]
a)Find the normalising constant \(c\). [1 Mark]
b)Compute the marginal PMFs \(P_X(x)\) and \(P_Y(y)\). [1.5 Marks]
c)Find \(P(X=1 \mid Y=1)\). [1 Mark]
d)Are \(X\) and \(Y\) independent? Justify. [0.5 Mark]
e)Compute \(E[X]\) and \(\text{Var}(X)\). [1 Mark]
✔️ Complete Solution
Joint PMF Table: f(x,y) = c(x+y)
y = 1y = 2P_X(x)
x = 0c(0+1)=cc(0+2)=2c3c
x = 1c(1+1)=2cc(1+2)=3c5c
x = 2c(2+1)=3cc(2+2)=4c7c
P_Y(y)6c9c15c
Part a — Find c
\[\sum_{x}\sum_{y} f(x,y) = 1 \implies 15c = 1 \implies \mathbf{c = \frac{1}{15}}\]
Part b — Marginal PMFs

Marginal of X: \(P_X(x) = \sum_y f(x,y)\)

\[P_X(0)=\frac{3}{15}=\frac{1}{5},\quad P_X(1)=\frac{5}{15}=\frac{1}{3},\quad P_X(2)=\frac{7}{15}\]

Marginal of Y: \(P_Y(y) = \sum_x f(x,y)\)

\[P_Y(1)=\frac{6}{15}=\frac{2}{5},\quad P_Y(2)=\frac{9}{15}=\frac{3}{5}\]
Part c — P(X=1 | Y=1)
\[P(X=1|Y=1) = \frac{P(X=1,Y=1)}{P_Y(1)} = \frac{2c}{6c} = \frac{2}{6} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{3}}\]
Part d — Independence Check

For independence: \(P(x,y) = P_X(x) \cdot P_Y(y)\) must hold for ALL x, y.

Check \(x=0,y=1\):

\(P(0,1) = \dfrac{1}{15}\)  but  \(P_X(0)\cdot P_Y(1) = \dfrac{1}{5} \times \dfrac{2}{5} = \dfrac{2}{25}\)

\(\dfrac{1}{15} \neq \dfrac{2}{25}\) → X and Y are NOT independent.

Part e — E[X] and Var(X)
\[E[X] = \sum x \cdot P_X(x) = 0\cdot\frac{1}{5} + 1\cdot\frac{1}{3} + 2\cdot\frac{7}{15} = 0 + \frac{5}{15} + \frac{14}{15} = \frac{19}{15} \approx 1.267\]
\[E[X^2] = 0^2\cdot\frac{1}{5} + 1^2\cdot\frac{1}{3} + 2^2\cdot\frac{7}{15} = 0 + \frac{5}{15} + \frac{28}{15} = \frac{33}{15} = \frac{11}{5} = 2.2\]
\[\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - [E[X]]^2 = 2.2 - \left(\frac{19}{15}\right)^2 = 2.2 - \frac{361}{225} = \frac{495-361}{225} = \frac{134}{225} \approx 0.596\]
✅ c=1/15 | P_X: 1/5, 1/3, 7/15 | P_Y: 2/5, 3/5 | P(X=1|Y=1)=1/3 | NOT independent | E[X]=19/15≈1.267 | Var(X)≈0.596

Q6 Distributions: Binomial, Poisson & CDF
5/6 Papers5 Marks

a) A data centre finds that 10% of its servers experience a minor fault each month. In a cluster of 20 servers, find the probability that exactly 2 servers fault this month using both the Binomial and Poisson distributions. Compare the results and comment on the accuracy of the Poisson approximation. [3 Marks]

b) The overheating index \(X\) of a cooling unit has CDF: \[F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \\ kx^3 & 0 \le x \le 2 \\ 1 & x > 2 \end{cases}\] Find:  (i) constant \(k\)   (ii) PDF \(f(x)\)   (iii) \(P(0.5 \le X \le 1.5)\) [2 Marks]

✔️ Complete Solution

Part (a) — Binomial vs Poisson

Given

\(n = 20,\quad p = 0.1,\quad k = 2\)

Binomial Distribution
\[P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\]
\[P(X=2) = \binom{20}{2}(0.1)^2(0.9)^{18}\]

\(\binom{20}{2} = \dfrac{20!}{2!\cdot18!} = 190\)

\((0.9)^{18}\):   \(0.9^2=0.81,\ 0.9^4=0.6561,\ 0.9^8=0.4305,\ 0.9^{16}=0.1853,\ 0.9^{18}=0.1853\times0.81=0.1501\)

\[P(X=2) = 190 \times 0.01 \times 0.1501 = \mathbf{0.2852}\]
Poisson Approximation

\(\lambda = np = 20 \times 0.1 = 2\)

\[P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}\]
\[P(X=2) = \frac{e^{-2}\cdot 2^2}{2!} = \frac{0.1353 \times 4}{2} = 0.1353 \times 2 = \mathbf{0.2707}\]
Comparison & Comment
MethodP(X=2)Difference
Binomial (exact)0.2852—
Poisson (approx)0.27070.0145

Comment: The Poisson approximation slightly underestimates the true probability. The approximation is best when \(n \ge 20\) and \(p \le 0.05\). Here \(p = 0.10\) is borderline, so the approximation is reasonably close but not exact.

Part (b) — CDF Analysis (New Trend Topic)

i) Find constant k

At \(x = 2\), CDF must equal 1:

\[F(2) = 1 \implies k \cdot (2)^3 = 1 \implies 8k = 1 \implies \mathbf{k = \frac{1}{8}}\]
ii) Derive PDF by differentiating CDF
\[f(x) = \frac{d}{dx}F(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^3}{8}\right) = \frac{3x^2}{8} \quad \text{for } 0 \le x \le 2\]

So: \(f(x) = \dfrac{3x^2}{8}\) for \(0 \le x \le 2\), and \(0\) otherwise.

iii) P(0.5 ≤ X ≤ 1.5) using CDF
\[P(0.5 \le X \le 1.5) = F(1.5) - F(0.5) = \frac{(1.5)^3}{8} - \frac{(0.5)^3}{8}\]
\[= \frac{3.375}{8} - \frac{0.125}{8} = \frac{3.25}{8} = \mathbf{0.40625}\]
✅ Binomial P(X=2)=0.2852 | Poisson P(X=2)=0.2707 | k=1/8 | f(x)=3x²/8 for 0≤x≤2 | P(0.5≤X≤1.5)=0.40625
